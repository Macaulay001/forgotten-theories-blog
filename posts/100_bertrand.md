# I tested Bertrand's 1887 ballot formula on 648 vote splits and the answer was always (p-q)/(p+q).

*Part 100 of **Forgotten Theories**, a series re-testing old scientific claims with modern tools. Find the rest under the `#forgotten-theories` tag.*

---

In 1887 Joseph Bertrand sent a four-page note to the *Comptes Rendus* of the French Academy. The question he asked is one you can pose to a child. Two candidates run for office. A wins with p votes, B loses with q < p. The ballots are pulled from the box in a uniformly random order and the running totals are announced. What is the probability that A is ahead of B at every single announcement, all the way from the first ballot to the last.

Bertrand stated the answer without proof:

    P(A always strictly ahead) = (p - q) / (p + q).

He wrote that the result "seems to merit attention". A few months later Désiré André found the now-classical proof by reflecting an offending path across the tie line. The same year, Émile Barbier and others published their own demonstrations. The whole thing fits inside a single half-page of careful counting and yet the answer is one of those formulas that looks too clean to be right. It only depends on the margin over the total. A 6-to-4 win and a 60-to-40 win produce the same "lead probability" of 0.2.

The result seeded the cycle lemma, the reflection principle, the Catalan-number identities used in lattice-path enumeration. It shows up in queueing theory and in nonparametric statistics. It also has a reputation, among people who teach it, for being unintuitive: students who can prove it still get surprised when they actually shuffle ballots and watch the numbers fall out.

I had quoted the formula for years without ever shuffling anything. So I shuffled.

## What I tried

The plan was a straight Monte Carlo. Pick a (p, q) pair with p > q. Build the sequence of p A-votes (call them +1) and q B-votes (call them -1). Shuffle it. Compute the running sum. Check that every partial sum is strictly positive. That last condition is exactly "A is strictly ahead at every step".

For one pair this is a one-line calculation. The interesting question is how the empirical probability behaves across a grid of (p, q). I picked every pair with 3 <= p + q <= 50 and p > q, which is 648 pairs.

The naive way (sample, shuffle, sum, check, repeat) was hopeless in plain Python: I let it run for ten minutes on the first try, watched it crawl, and killed it. Vectorising fixed everything. For each pair, draw an (n_trials, n) matrix of uniform random keys, argsort each row to get a random permutation, index the +/-1 vector by that permutation, take the cumulative sum along the row axis, and count the rows whose minimum is still positive. NumPy does the hard work in C.

I ran 100,000 trials per pair. At p + q = 50 that is a 50 megabyte temporary array per pair, which fits in cache, and the whole sweep finished in a couple of minutes.

A side check uses the cycle lemma. When p = q + 1 the formula collapses to 1 / (2q + 1). Multiplied by the (p+q choose q) total orderings, that should give the q-th Catalan number C_q. This is an exact-arithmetic identity, not a Monte Carlo one, so it is a useful sanity check on the formula itself rather than on the sampler.

## What happened

The match is the sort of match that makes you suspicious that the experiment is rigged. Across the 648 pairs, the largest absolute deviation between empirical fraction and (p-q)/(p+q) is 0.0042, and the root-mean-square deviation is 0.0013. The theoretical sampling standard error on a single proportion at p = 0.5 and n = 100,000 is sqrt(0.25 / 100,000) = 0.0016. The observed RMSE is, if anything, slightly *below* the predicted standard error, because most of the probabilities being estimated are not at 0.5.

Here is the core of the simulator. No imports, no boilerplate, just the loop body:

```python
base = np.empty(p + q, dtype=np.int8)
base[:p], base[p:] = 1, -1

keys = rng.random((n_trials, p + q))
order = np.argsort(keys, axis=1)
seqs = base[order]                       # +/-1 along each row
cum = np.cumsum(seqs, axis=1)
always = np.all(cum > 0, axis=1)
p_emp = always.mean()
```

A slice through the data at fixed total p + q = 40 looks like this:

| p  | q  | theory | empirical |
|----|----|--------|-----------|
| 39 | 1  | 0.9500 | 0.9495 |
| 30 | 10 | 0.5000 | 0.5004 |
| 25 | 15 | 0.2500 | 0.2488 |
| 21 | 19 | 0.0500 | 0.0502 |

Every entry matches in the third decimal. The right column was generated by shuffling, the middle column by long division.

The left panel plots empirical against theoretical across all 648 pairs. Every point sits on y = x within the error bar. The right panel is a (p, q) grid coloured by the signed residual; it looks like noise around zero with no pattern in p or q. The formula does what it says it does.

The Catalan side-check passed cleanly. When p = q + 1, the count of ballot orderings in which A is strictly ahead the whole way is, for q = 1..8:

    1, 2, 5, 14, 42, 132, 429, 1430

which is the Catalan sequence. This is not Monte Carlo; it is exact integer arithmetic from the formula, and it confirms the half of Bertrand's result that bridges combinatorics and probability. Each Catalan ballot path is also a Dyck path, an admissible sequence of opening and closing parentheses, a triangulation of a convex polygon. The same number, six different stories.

## What it may mean

Three things stand out. The first is that the answer truly does depend only on (p - q) / (p + q). A 51-49 squeaker and a 5100-4900 squeaker have the same chance, around 2%, of holding the lead every step. The intuition often offered, that bigger elections somehow "average out" early swings, is wrong on this measure. The margin matters; the size does not.

The second is the cycle-lemma flavour of the proof. It says: among the (p + q) cyclic rotations of any ballot sequence, exactly (p - q) of them have A strictly ahead throughout. So the probability across all rotations is (p - q) / (p + q), and because random rotations of random shuffles are still random shuffles, that is the answer over all shuffles. This kind of bijective argument is now standard in combinatorics, but in 1887 it was genuinely new.

The third is more practical. The same proof gives the joint distribution of partial sums in any random walk with fixed step counts, which is the discrete cousin of Brownian-bridge boundary crossing. The formula for "lead probability" is, up to translation, the formula for the maximum of a discrete bridge. That is why it shows up in nonparametric goodness-of-fit tests, where one tracks the running difference between an empirical and a reference distribution.

None of this is news to anyone who has read the first chapter of Feller. But running the experiment in a few minutes on a laptop gives a feel that no proof can quite reproduce. The numbers come out right. They keep coming out right as you sweep p and q. The 19th-century formula is doing what 19th-century formulas occasionally do: standing up to a billion shuffles without apology.

## Loose ends

I tested the strictly-ahead version. A weakly-ahead variant, where ties are allowed, has its own formula, (p - q + 1) / (p + 1), which I would like to confirm the same way. There is also a beautiful generalisation due to Lyle Takács in 1962 that handles k-candidate ballots, and a less-known version where the votes are non-integer weights. The weighted case is harder to test by shuffling because the "lead" condition becomes a stopping-time question rather than a parity one. That seems like the next afternoon's work.
