# I counted vertices, edges, and faces for 15 solids and a few random meshes. Euler's 1758 formula held in every case.

*Part 94 of **Forgotten Theories**, a series re-testing old scientific claims with modern tools. Find the rest under the `#forgotten-theories` tag.*

---

Pick up a die. It has 8 corners, 12 edges, and 6 faces. Add those
with alternating signs and you get 8 - 12 + 6 = 2. Try a tetrahedron:
4 - 6 + 4 = 2. The octahedron: 6 - 12 + 8 = 2. The number sits there
across all five Platonic solids, and it sits there for the
Archimedean solids too, and it sits there for any convex polyhedron
you care to construct. Leonhard Euler noticed this in 1758 and
wrote down what is, on the surface, a child's arithmetic identity.

It isn't a child's identity. The quantity V - E + F is what modern
topology calls the Euler characteristic, written chi. It is a
topological invariant: bend the polyhedron, stretch a face,
subdivide an edge, replace the whole skeleton with a different
triangulation. The number does not move. On a sphere it is 2. On
a torus (one handle) it drops to 0. On a double torus (two handles)
it falls to -2. The general law for closed orientable surfaces is
chi = 2 - 2g, where g is the genus, the number of handles.

Most introductions stop there. I wanted to sit with the formula on
a screen and watch it work, both on named solids whose counts I
could write by hand and on triangulations generated by code where
no human had checked V, E, and F.

## What I tried

The plan came in three pieces. First, hand-encode the (V, E, F)
triple for the five Platonic solids and ten Archimedean solids,
including the truncated cuboctahedron with its 48 vertices and
the truncated icosahedron, which is the panel layout of a classical
soccer ball. Compute V - E + F for each. The expectation is 2 every
time.

Second, generate random triangulations of the 2-sphere. The trick
is small. Sample n points uniformly on the unit sphere by drawing
3D Gaussian vectors and normalising, then take the 3D convex hull.
For points on a sphere the convex hull is automatically a Delaunay
triangulation, and it tessellates the sphere with triangles. Count
vertices (n), faces (the number of triangles in the hull), and
edges (extract from the triangle list as unordered pairs). Run this
at n = 20, 50, 100, 500, 2000. If Euler's formula is a topological
statement and not a quirk of named solids, chi should land on 2
regardless of n or the random seed.

Third, do the same for a torus. Build a quad grid in (u, v)
parameter space, wrap both directions periodically, place each grid
node on the torus surface in 3D, then split each quad into two
triangles. Count carefully (the periodic identification means
indices wrap modulo nu and nv). The expected number is chi = 0.

The interesting thing about the audit is not whether it confirms
Euler. It will. The interesting thing is the texture of how it
confirms him: which counts come out, what pattern they fall into,
what the numbers say about the structure of a triangulated sphere
versus a triangulated torus.

## What happened

Across all 15 named solids, every count gives V - E + F = 2. No
exceptions. The truncated cuboctahedron has V = 48, E = 72, F = 26,
and 48 - 72 + 26 = 2. The snub cube has V = 24, E = 60, F = 38, and
24 - 60 + 38 = 2. The truncated icosahedron has V = 60, E = 90,
F = 32 (12 pentagons + 20 hexagons), and 60 - 90 + 32 = 2.

Random spherical triangulations behaved as expected. Here is the
core of the count. The convex hull of points on a sphere gives a
triangulation; each triangle contributes three undirected edges,
and edges shared between two triangles are deduplicated by sorting.

```python
pts = rng.normal(size=(n, 3))
pts /= np.linalg.norm(pts, axis=1, keepdims=True)
hull = ConvexHull(pts)
edges = set()
for a, b, c in hull.simplices:
    a, b, c = sorted((a, b, c))
    edges.update([(a, b), (b, c), (a, c)])
V, E, F = len(pts), len(edges), len(hull.simplices)
print(V, E, F, V - E + F)
```

The output for n = 500 is V = 500, E = 1494, F = 996, chi = 2. For
n = 2000 it is V = 2000, E = 5994, F = 3996, chi = 2. A pattern
emerges if you stare at the rows: F = 2V - 4 and E = 3V - 6. Those
are the standard relations for a triangulation of a sphere, and
plugging them back in gives V - (3V - 6) + (2V - 4) = 2 algebraically.

The torus meshes confirmed the second half of the formula. A 24-by-14
periodic quad grid (triangulated into 672 triangles) gave V = 336,
E = 1008, F = 672, and chi = 0. A finer 40-by-24 grid gave V = 960,
E = 2880, F = 1920, and again chi = 0. The torus has genus 1, so
chi = 2 - 2(1) = 0. The arithmetic agrees.

## What it may mean

The audit is not a proof. Euler had a proof; Cauchy, Legendre, and
von Staudt later sharpened it; the modern statement sits inside
algebraic topology and is several layers more general than what
Euler wrote in 1758. What the audit shows is that the topological
invariance is operational: pick any of these surfaces, count by
hand or count by code, and the number does not move.

There is a small wrinkle worth flagging. Euler's original phrasing
applied to convex polyhedra, and it broke when topologists started
considering polyhedra with tunnels (a cube with a square hole
through the middle has chi = 0, not 2). The history of the formula,
recounted in Lakatos's *Proofs and Refutations* (1976), is largely
the history of finding objects that violate the original statement
and then refining the statement until the violators became cases
of the general 2 - 2g law. The version we tested is the refined
one.

The downstream consequences are large. The hairy ball theorem
follows from chi(S^2) = 2 not being zero. The Gauss-Bonnet theorem
says that the integral of Gaussian curvature over a closed surface
equals 2*pi*chi, which means total curvature is a topological
invariant. In chemistry, a closed cage of carbon atoms made from
pentagons and hexagons must contain exactly 12 pentagons; that
number, 12, is forced by chi = 2 and the local valences. The C60
buckyball is the smallest such cage. Euler's identity is what makes
the buckyball's pentagon count nonnegotiable.

## Loose ends

The audit covers genus 0 and genus 1. A natural next test would
generate random genus-g surfaces by attaching g handles to a sphere
and confirming chi tracks 2 - 2g across g = 0, 1, 2, 3. That extension
takes another hour of code. The other thing left undone is the
non-orientable case: a Mobius strip and a Klein bottle have their
own characteristics (1 and 0 respectively), and the general formula
is chi = 2 - k where k counts crosscaps. Euler did not consider
these surfaces; topology did, and the identity survived the
generalization.
